# Māyā-Tattva (Śākta) -- माया

**Not:** illusion (the standard mistranslation), the Advaita māyā (one concept across schools), Mahāmāyā / the cosmological māyā (v0.7), deception / trickery (moral reading)

## What It Actually Means

In the 36-tattva schema (see [shattrimshat-tattva.md](shattrimshat-tattva.md)), māyā is tattva number six: the hinge between the five pure tattvas (consciousness's internal articulation) and everything limited. Its function is precise: it is the power of differentiation, breaking the absolute's "I am this-all" into "I" versus "this," and, through its five kañcukas (see [kanchuka.md](kanchuka.md)), scaling infinite capacities down to individual dimensions. The śuddhāśuddha ("pure-impure") label captures its Janus position: viewed from above, māyā is Śakti's own free operation; viewed from below, it is the horizon of every limitation a subject experiences.

The doctrinally loaded point, and the reason this file must never be merged with its cross-bundle cousins: here māyā is real, and so are its products. Śākta-Trika metaphysics defines itself against illusionism: if the world were unreal, Śakti's self-expression would be a pretense, and the tradition's entire theology of manifestation (see [shakti.md](shakti.md), [spanda.md](spanda.md)) would collapse. Where Advaita reads the rope-snake, this tradition reads a dancer's gesture: really her, really moving, really many shapes, one dancer.

## Audience Metaphor

Māyā-tattva is a hypervisor: the layer that partitions one machine into many virtual machines, each with capped cores, fenced memory, and its own clock. The hypervisor is not a malfunction and not an illusion; it is real software doing exactly its job, and every VM's finitude is its work. Advaita's māyā-question ("is the VM real?") is a different debate in a different architecture; in THIS architecture, the answer is yes: real partition, real machine, one substrate.
