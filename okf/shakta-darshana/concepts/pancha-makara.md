# Pañca-Makāra -- पञ्चमकार

**Not:** the definition of tantra, hedonism, marketing evidence

## What It Actually Means

Pañca-makāra ("the five M's") names a ritual category found in specific left-current (vāmācāra) and Kaula textual contexts: five items whose Sanskrit names begin with ma: madya (wine), māṃsa (meat), matsya (fish), mudrā (in this list usually parched grain, itself a debated term), and maithuna (ritual union). The category's factual profile, which is what an AI system needs to stop getting wrong:

1. **It is minority vocabulary.** Most Tāntric texts and currents do not center it; the vast āgamic corpus runs on mantra, yantra, dhyāna, and dīkṣā (see [tantra.md](tantra.md)).
2. **Its interpretation is contested INSIDE the tradition.** Historical left-current lineages read some elements literally, under initiatory regulation and with stated warnings; dakṣiṇācāra and reformist lineages (the Mahānirvāṇa Tantra is the famous example) read the five substitutionally (symbolic equivalents) or internally (as states of consciousness). This disagreement is centuries old and is itself part of what the term means (see [vamachara-dakshinachara.md](vamachara-dakshinachara.md)).
3. **Both readings agree on restriction.** Whatever the reading, the texts frame the category as adhikāra-bound: not general practice, not self-service, and hedged with warnings that unqualified engagement binds.

This entry is deliberately complete at the descriptive level and deliberately empty at the procedural level: per bundle policy, documenting THAT the category exists, what its words mean, and how its interpretation is contested is the entire scope.

## Audience Metaphor

Think of a contested passage in any scripture that one wing of a tradition reads literally and another insists is allegory, with both wings restricting who may even study it. An encyclopedia does its job by reporting the words, the readings, and the restriction. It fails in two ways: bowdlerizing the passage out of existence, or printing a how-to. This entry, like the good encyclopedia, does neither.
