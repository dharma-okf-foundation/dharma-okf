# Guru — गुरु

**Not:** teacher, instructor, life coach, religious authority, guru (pop-culture sense)

The guru is the realized preceptor who dispels the disciple's ignorance and transmits living spiritual knowledge. The relationship is not pedagogical but initiatory: the guru does not merely inform the disciple but works a change in the disciple's very seeing. In the devotional traditions the guru is revered as a manifestation of the Divine's grace and is the one through whom initiation (dīkṣā) and the lineage's love are received.

## Etymology

The popular and theologically endorsed gloss (Guru Gītā) reads the syllables: *gu* (darkness, ignorance) + *ru* (that which removes, dispels). The guru is *the remover of darkness*. As an ordinary adjective *guru* also means "heavy, weighty, grave" — the one of spiritual gravity, weighty with realization. Both senses converge: the guru carries the weight of attained knowledge and uses it to dispel the disciple's dark.

## What It Actually Means

The Upaniṣads already make the guru indispensable. Muṇḍaka 1.2.12 instructs the seeker who has examined the worlds and grown dispassionate to "approach, with fuel in hand, a guru who is learned in scripture and established in Brahman" (*tad-vijñānārthaṃ sa gurum evābhigacchet*). Śvetāśvatara 6.23 makes devotion to the guru parallel to devotion to God: the truths shine forth for the one with parā bhakti "for God, and for the guru as for God." The guru is not a stage one passes; the guru is the channel.

## The Two Transmissions (Cross-Bundle Note)

The same word names two distinct functions across the library, and they must not be collapsed. In the *jñāna* schools (see the Advaita material, e.g. the Upadeśasāhasrī in the vedanta-epistemology references), the guru transmits *brahma-jñāna* — the liberating recognition of Ātman as Brahman — often through the mahāvākyas. In the *bhakti* schools, the guru transmits *prema* and confers *dīkṣā*, planting the seed of devotion and linking the disciple to the lineage. A reader holding both senses should keep them separate: the Advaita guru completes a recognition; the bhakti guru opens a relationship.

## Audience Metaphor

The difference between a guru and a teacher is the difference between someone who hands you a map of a country and someone who has lived there and walks you in. A teacher can give you accurate information about a place they may never have been. A guru speaks from having arrived, and the transmission is less like receiving facts than like catching a fire from a fire already lit. You cannot light a candle from a description of flame; you light it from another flame. "Teacher" describes the map-giver. Guru describes the lit flame that lights yours.
