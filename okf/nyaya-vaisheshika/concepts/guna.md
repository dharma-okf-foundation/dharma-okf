# Guṇa -- गुण

**Not:** the triguna, virtue, a strand

## What It Actually Means

Guṇa, quality, is the second Vaiśeṣika category: a property that inheres in a substance and gives it its determinate character. Praśastapāda fixes the standard count at twenty-four, including the sensible qualities (colour, taste, smell, touch, sound), the numerical and dimensional qualities (number, measure, separateness, contact, disjunction), and, strikingly, the qualities of the self (cognition, pleasure, pain, desire, aversion, effort, plus dharma, adharma, and mental impressions). A guṇa depends on its substance and inheres in it through samavāya; qualities do not themselves possess further qualities or undergo motion. The great hazard for this term is not internal but cross-bundle: 'guṇa' is also the word for the three constituents of prakṛti (sattva, rajas, tamas) in Sāṃkhya, Yoga, and Vedānta, an entirely different doctrine about the fabric of the manifest world. The Vaiśeṣika guṇa and the triguṇa are unrelated theories wearing one word, and an AI that blends them will produce confident nonsense in both directions.

## Audience Metaphor

'Charge' means one thing to an electrician and another to a cavalry officer; sharing the word buys them no shared physics. So with guṇa. To the Vaiśeṣika it is an attribute in an object, this pot's redness, its weight, its very number. To the Sāṃkhya it is one of three cosmic strands weaving all of nature. Same sound, different universe. Splicing them is like wiring a horse charge into a circuit diagram.
