# Anubhava — अनुभव

**Not:** experience (generic phenomenological), intuition (Kantian or Bergsonian), feeling (emotional register), perception (as adequate translation), empirical experience

**Anubhava** is the Sanskrit epistemological term for direct cognition — a cognition that is not a recollection of a prior cognition but a fresh cognitive event, produced directly by contact with its object (or, in the case of liberative jñāna, by the Mahāvākya pointing to the non-objective Brahman). In Advaita Vedānta's epistemological framework, anubhava plays a double role: (1) at the vyāvahārika level, it is the general term for any fresh non-mnemonic cognition produced by the pramāṇas; (2) at the pāramārthika level, it names the criterion of authenticity for brahma-jñāna — the direct recognition that is not conceptual understanding-about but genuine aparokṣa-anubhūti (immediate non-mediated realization).

The Brahmasūtra Bhāṣya 1.1.2 states that Vedānta inquiry (*brahma-jijñāsā*) has *anubhava* as its terminus — *anubhavāvasānatvāt*. This is a central epistemological claim: the process of studying scripture, engaging teachers, and engaging in śravaṇa-manana-nididhyāsana culminates in anubhava — direct recognition — not in a refined conceptual belief. Without anubhava, the process is incomplete.

## Etymology

From Sanskrit *anu* (following, after; or along, according to — prefix with multiple senses depending on context) + *bhava* (from root *bhū*, to become, to be; noun: being, occurrence, arising). Literally: what arises along with (the cognitive event); what follows or accompanies cognition.

The nominal form *anubhūti* (stronger, more abstract) is used in compounds like *aparokṣa-anubhūti* (immediate, non-mediated realization). Both *anubhava* and *anubhūti* are used in Advaita literature; *anubhūti* tends to appear in soteriological contexts and *anubhava* in broader epistemological ones.

## Anubhava in the Pramāṇa Framework

At the vyāvahārika level, anubhava is the general term for any cognition produced by the six pramāṇas that is not a recollection. The Vedāntaparibhāṣā (Ch.1) uses this as the technical definition: anubhava = any non-mnemonic cognition (*asmṛti*). This distinguishes fresh pramāṇa-results from memory (smṛti), which merely reactivates a prior cognition without producing a new epistemic contact.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Anubhava** | Fresh cognition — not a recollection; produced by pramāṇa-event | Seeing the fire (pratyakṣa); inferring it from smoke (anumāna) |
| **Smṛti** (memory) | Recollection of a prior cognition | Remembering the fire you saw yesterday |
| **Aparokṣa-anubhūti** | Direct, non-mediated anubhava — the liberative form | Recognition of ātman = Brahman via Mahāvākya |

The distinction from smṛti is philosophically important in the Advaita-Mīmāṃsā debate about the authority of Vedic testimony: if śabda (Vedic testimony) merely recalled prior human knowledge, it would be smṛti rather than anubhava, and its authority would derive from its original human source (paratahprāmāṇya). The Mīmāṃsā-Advaita position: apauruṣeya Śruti is not a recollection at all — it is anubhava (or the direct vehicle of anubhava), and its validity is svataḥprāmāṇya.

## Two Functions of Anubhava in Advaita

### Function 1: Epistemological Criterion (Vyāvahārika Level)

Anubhava serves as the baseline cognitive category against which all Advaita epistemology is measured. A claim is epistemically grounded when it traces back to anubhava — either a direct perceptual event, a valid inferential conclusion, or an Upaniṣadic transmission.

This is why Advaita commentators invoke anubhava as a check against over-reliance on scripture-study alone: śāstra-vākya (scriptural statements) must be heard, reflected upon, and ultimately produce anubhava — direct recognition. Without anubhava, the scriptural statements remain parokṣa.

### Function 2: Liberative Criterion (Pāramārthika Level)

At the pāramārthika level, anubhava is the criterion for distinguishing genuine brahma-jñāna from its approximations:

- **Śravaṇa** (hearing the Mahāvākyas) produces parokṣa-jñāna: understanding-about brahma-jñāna. This is not yet anubhava in the full sense.
- **Manana** (reflection, resolving doubts) deepens the parokṣa-jñāna but does not produce anubhava.
- **Nididhyāsana** (contemplation) is the practice that ripens parokṣa into aparokṣa — that converts conceptual understanding into anubhava.
- **Aparokṣa-anubhūti** — the immediate realization — is anubhava in its liberative form: the direct recognition of ātman-Brahman identity that constitutes mokṣa.

Vivekacūḍāmaṇi v.5 makes this explicit: scripture study (*śāstrādhyayana*) is preparation; the realization itself is anubhava. A student who has read all the Upaniṣads and can explain advaita perfectly but has not had the anubhava has not yet reached the goal. The goal is not intellectual mastery but direct recognition.

## Anubhava and Adhyāsa

The relationship between anubhava and adhyāsa is the key to Advaita's epistemological logic:

- **Adhyāsa** (superimposition) produces mithyā cognitions: the snake-cognition in poor light is a cognition that *appears* to be anubhava (it is not smṛti — you are not recalling a past snake) but is produced by a doṣa-infected instrument (poor lighting + prior conditioning). It is a case of pseudo-anubhava — it has the phenomenological structure of fresh cognition but is produced by avidyā-tinged conditions.
- **Genuine anubhava** at the vyāvahārika level is produced without doṣa — adequate light, well-functioning sense organs, correct conditions. The rope-cognition in good light is genuine anubhava.
- **Brahma-jñāna as anubhava** removes the root adhyāsa — not a particular instance of superimposition (like the snake) but the fundamental superimposition of ātman with body-mind-world. The anubhava that removes adhyāsa is the aparokṣa-anubhūti.

## The Paradox of Trans-Objective Anubhava

Anubhava typically involves a cognizer having direct contact with an object. But brahma-jñāna, as anubhava, is paradoxical: Brahman is not an object of cognition (it is the cognizer's own nature), and the cognizer (jīva) is itself mithyā from the pāramārthika perspective. So what is the structure of this anubhava?

**Advaita's answer:** The anubhava of brahma-jñāna dissolves the cognizer-cognized structure rather than completing it. It is the recognition that the structure was always superimposed on what is actually non-dual Brahman. The language of "anubhava" is vyāvahārika language applied to a pāramārthika recognition — the only language available within the frame that is being dissolved. After the recognition, there is no residual experiencer having an experience of Brahman; there is only Brahman. The anubhava-language is like the stick used to stir the funeral pyre that is then consumed in the same fire.

## Anubhava Across Schools

Mīmāṃsā, Nyāya, and Advaita each use anubhava, but with distinct emphases:

| School | Primary use of anubhava | Key distinction |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Mīmāṃsā** | Cognition that precedes memory; used to establish that Vedic cognition is not smṛti and therefore has independent authority | Focused on the smṛti/anubhava distinction for Vedic apauruṣeya |
| **Nyāya** | General term for any non-mnemonic cognition, valid or invalid; pramā is the subset that is valid | Neutral — includes both valid and invalid cognitions |
| **Advaita** | Both the general epistemological usage AND the liberative criterion; anubhava is what śravaṇa-manana-nididhyāsana aims at; aparokṣa-anubhūti is brahma-jñāna | Extends anubhava from the vyāvahārika to the pāramārthika level |

## Audience Metaphor

There is knowing that fire burns from a physics textbook, knowing it from watching someone get burned, and knowing it because you touched the stove. These are all 'knowing that fire burns' — but the epistemic quality differs markedly. Anubhava is the third kind: the knowing that is inseparable from the event, where the distinction between knowing and what is known collapses. Brahma-jñāna as anubhava goes further still: not merely 'I directly cognized Brahman' (which would still leave a cognizer facing Brahman-as-object) but 'the cognizer recognized itself as Brahman' — the event dissolves the gap between knower and known that made it look like an event at all.
