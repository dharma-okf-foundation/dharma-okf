# Pramā — प्रमा

**Not:** truth (propositional), justified true belief (JTB), knowledge (generic English), belief (doxastic), correct perception, apramā (invalid cognition)

**Pramā** is the Sanskrit epistemological term for **veridical cognition** — the valid result produced when a pramāṇa (instrument of valid cognition) operates on a prameya (object of cognition) and delivers a cognition that corresponds to its object. It is the *phala* (result, outcome) of the pramāṇa-triad: pramātṛ (knowing subject) + pramāṇa (instrument) + prameya (object) → pramā (valid cognition).

The term appears in its most technically precise form in the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika and Mīmāṃsā traditions but is foundational to Advaita Vedānta's epistemological architecture as well. The Tattvabodha (attributed to Śaṅkara) introduces the pramāṇa-triad as the basic structure of Advaita epistemology: the student must know how the instruments of valid cognition work before understanding how brahma-jñāna — the liberative pramā — operates within and ultimately overturns that framework.

## Etymology

From Sanskrit *pra-* (prefix: forward, complete, fully) + *māna* (from root *mā*, to measure, to cognize) → *pramā*: a completed, fully effected cognition; a cognition that has measured (arrived at) its object as it actually is. The *pra-* prefix distinguishes *pramā* (successful, veridical cognition) from mere *māna* (measuring attempt) and from *apramā* (invalid, non-corresponding cognition; *a-pramā* — the negation).

## The Pramāṇa-Triad and Pramā as Phala

Advaita Vedānta (following Mīmāṃsā and Nyāya) analyzes every valid epistemic event into four structural elements:

| Element | Sanskrit | Role |
|---------|----------|------|
| **Knower** | Pramātṛ | The cognitive subject; the witness-awareness operating through the antaḥkaraṇa (mind-complex) |
| **Instrument** | Pramāṇa | The means by which the object is cognized: pratyakṣa, anumāna, śabda, upamāna, arthāpatti, anupalabdhi |
| **Object** | Prameya | What is cognized; the domain of the pramāṇa; differs by pramāṇa type |
| **Valid cognition** | Pramā | The result: the cognition that corresponds to its object |

Pramā is thus not the pramāṇa itself (the instrument) but the cognitive result produced when the instrument functions correctly on its object. The distinction matters: a valid pramāṇa that is obstructed by doṣa (defect) does not produce pramā. A defective instrument (miscalibrated sense organ, doṣa-laden mind) produces apramā even when the object is present.

## Definition: Three Conditions for Pramā

The Vedāntaparibhāṣā Ch.1 builds the Advaita definition of pramā from three conditions:

**Condition 1: Anubhava (non-mnemonic cognition)** — pramā must be a fresh cognitive event, not a recollection. Smṛti (memory of "the fire I saw yesterday") is not pramā even if accurate, because it is recollection of a prior cognition, not a new epistemic contact.

**Condition 2: Yathārthatva (object-correspondence)** — the cognition must correspond to its object: it must cognize the object as it actually is, not as other than it is (anyathākhyāti). Cognizing the rope as a snake is anyathā-khyāti — cognizing something as other than it is — and is therefore apramā.

**Condition 3: Non-sublation (abādhitatva)** — the cognition must not be subsequently sublated (bādhita) by a higher or stronger cognition. A cognition of silver in a shell (śukti-rajata) appears veridical until closer inspection reveals the shell: the shell-silver cognition is retroactively bādhita and revealed as apramā. A cognition that survives all subsequent investigation is pramā.

## Pramā and Svataḥprāmāṇya

The Advaita-Mīmāṃsā position (see svataḥpramanya.md) holds that pramā is self-validating (*svataḥprāmāṇya*): the validity of a pramā is intrinsic to the cognition itself, not dependent on external confirmation. When a pramā arises (say, the cognition "this is fire"), its validity is not produced by a subsequent meta-cognition ("my cognition of fire is valid"); the fire-cognition is self-warranted by its arising.

This is in contrast to the Nyāya position (parataḥprāmāṇya): that the validity of a cognition requires external confirmation — a subsequent checking-cognition or the track record of the instrument. The Advaita-Mīmāṃsā argument: if validity required an external validator, that validator would itself require validation, producing an infinite regress; svataḥprāmāṇya blocks the regress.

The practical consequence: a pramā needs no independent justification. It is valid until bādhita. The burden of proof falls on the bādha, not on the pramā.

## Pramā vs. Apramā: The Advaita Soteriological Stakes

The pramā/apramā distinction carries enormous soteriological weight in Advaita:

**Avidyā as the source of systematic apramā:** Every cognition of the jīva as a separate self in an independently real world is, from the pāramārthika standpoint, apramā — it cognizes the appearance (mithyā jīva, mithyā jagat) as if it were the ultimately real. The cognition "I am this body-mind" is apramā because the body-mind is not the ultimate reality of the cognizer.

**Brahma-jñāna as the definitive pramā:** The liberative cognition "aham brahmāsmi" (I am Brahman) is the pramā that bādhita-s all prior apramās about the self. It corresponds to the object (Brahman, which is the actual nature of the cognizer) without the distortion of avidyā, meets the three conditions (it is anubhava; it is yathārtha — it corresponds to Brahman as the actual reality; it is the trikālābādhita cognition, never sublatable), and is self-validating.

**The paradox:** From the vyāvahārika standpoint, brahma-jñāna is a pramā — a valid cognition produced by the śabda-pramāṇa (Mahāvākya-śabda) on the prameya (Brahman). From the pāramārthika standpoint, brahma-jñāna dissolves the pramātṛ-pramāṇa-prameya-pramā structure itself — because Brahman is neither subject to knowing nor an object of knowing but the awareness in which all such structures appear. The pramā framework is vyāvahārika scaffolding that is dismantled in the very act of successful brahma-jñāna.

## Pramā Across the Six Pramāṇas

Each pramāṇa produces pramā within its domain:

| Pramāṇa | Domain | Example pramā |
|---------|--------|---------------|
| Pratyakṣa | Perceptible present objects | "This fire is hot" (tactile-thermal) |
| Anumāna | Inferrable facts via liṅga | "There is fire on that hill" (inferring from smoke) |
| Śabda / Āgama | Testimony of valid speakers; Vedic statements | "Ātman is Brahman" (Mahāvākya-śabda) |
| Upamāna | Analogical cognition: similarity-based | "The wild cow is like a domestic cow" |
| Arthāpatti | Postulation from incongruent facts | "Devadatta must eat at night" (because he is fat but eats nothing by day) |
| Anupalabdhi | Cognition from non-apprehension | "There is no pot on the table" |

The pramā produced by each pramāṇa carries the scope and authority of that pramāṇa. Pratyakṣa-produced pramā covers the domain of sensible present objects. Śabda-produced pramā covers what language can validly transmit — including, in the Mīmāṃsā-Advaita view, the Mahāvākyas' transmission of the ātman-Brahman identity.

## The Vyāvahārika Persistence of Pramā After Aparokṣa-anubhūti

A key Advaita position: after aparokṣa-anubhūti, the jīvanmukta continues to operate in the vyāvahārika world, which means the ordinary pramāṇas continue to produce ordinary pramās. The jīvanmukta sees fire, infers rain from clouds, hears instruction. These pramās operate normally. What has changed is the root context: these pramās no longer occur within the superimposed framework of "I am a separate self in an independently real world." The jīvanmukta has the pramā "this is fire" without the accompanying adhyāsic context "and I am a body that can be burned by this fire as an ultimate ontological reality."

The vyāvahārika pramās remain; the adhyāsic metadata that surrounded them is removed.

## Audience Metaphor

A surveyor's measurement of a plot of land is pramā if it corresponds to the actual dimensions of the plot — the measurement instrument (theodolite, measuring tape) worked correctly, the conditions were adequate, and the result matches the land. An incorrect measurement (instrument miscalibrated, tape kinked) is apramā — it does not correspond to the object. The Advaita move: the surveyor's measurements of the jīva ('this body-mind complex is my self') and the world ('these objects are independently real') are systematic apramā, produced not by kinked tape but by avidyā. Brahma-jñāna is the survey that finally measures correctly — and in measuring correctly, discovers that the entire territory was Brahman, and the surveyor was too.
