# Puruṣa Sūkta -- पुरुष सूक्त

**Not:** a literal sacrifice account, the origin of birth-based caste, exclusively a Viṣṇava text

## Structure and Dating

The Puruṣa Sūkta (RV 10.90) is one of the most celebrated and most analyzed hymns in Vedic literature. It is generally regarded by scholars as a late addition to the Ṛg Veda (Book 10 is considered later than most of the other books) and as one of the foundational cosmological and social texts of the Vedic tradition. The hymn has 24 verses; the consensus among text historians is that the original hymn had 16 and 8 were added later.

The hymn is found with variations in:
- Ṛg Veda 10.90 (16 verses in the oldest layer)
- Śukla Yajurveda 31 (most widely used liturgical version)
- Atharva Veda 19.6

## The Cosmic Puruṣa

The hymn opens with a description of the Puruṣa's cosmic magnitude:
- He has a thousand heads (sahasraśīrṣā), a thousand eyes (sahasrākṣa), a thousand feet (sahasrapāt)
- He encompasses the entire earth on all sides and extends ten fingers beyond
- Three-quarters of him is immortal in the heavens; one-quarter is here in the manifest world

This is not anatomy but cosmological proportion: the manifest cosmos is only one-quarter of the Puruṣa's total reality. Three-quarters remains unmanifest, transcendent. This encoding maps onto the Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad's turīya framework: three states (waking/dreaming/sleep = three-quarters manifest) and the fourth (turīya = the transcendent three-quarters that doesn't enter manifestation).

## The Cosmic Sacrifice and Differentiation

The central section describes a cosmic sacrifice in which the primordial Puruṣa is the offering:

- From his mind (manas) was born the moon (Candra)
- From his eye was born the sun (Sūrya)
- From his mouth: Indra and Agni
- From his breath (prāṇa): Vāyu
- From his navel: the mid-space (antarikṣa)
- From his head: the sky (dyaus)
- From his feet: the earth (bhūmi)
- From his ear: the directions

This is a macrocosm-microcosm homology: the cosmic body generates the cosmos's elements. The same structure recurs in BhP 2.1.23-39 (the Virāṭ as cosmic body). The cosmos is the Puruṣa made external.

## The Varṇa Verses

Verses 11-12 of the Puruṣa Sūkta state:
- From his mouth: Brāhmaṇa
- From his two arms: Rājanya (Kṣatriya)
- From his thighs: Vaiśya
- From his feet: Śūdra

These verses have been the most cited and most misread in the entire Vedic corpus. The correct cosmological reading: varṇa is presented as a cosmic function-differentiation, not a social hierarchy. Mouth = speech/teaching function; arms = protection/rule function; thighs = economic/production function; feet = service/foundation function. The Puruṣa's body is complete only when all four are present and functioning. None is independent of the others.

The misreading: using these verses to legitimate a birth-based hereditary caste system. This conflates varṇa (functional role in cosmic and social order) with jāti (birth-community). The Puruṣa Sūkta does not mention jāti. The conflation happened gradually over centuries of Dharmaśāstra literature. Any AI system that cites the Puruṣa Sūkta as Vedic sanction for hereditary caste hierarchy is repeating a post-Vedic conflation.

## Audience Metaphor

The Puruṣa Sūkta's cosmic sacrifice is like the sun emitting light: the sun does not diminish when it gives light to the world; the light IS the sun in a different mode. The Puruṣa "sacrificed" into the cosmos has not ceased to exist -- three-quarters of the Puruṣa remains transcendent while one-quarter is expressed as the manifest cosmos. The world is not the Puruṣa's corpse; it is the Puruṣa's expression.
