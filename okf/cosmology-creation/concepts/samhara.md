# Saṃhāra / Laya — संहार / लय

**Not:** destruction, annihilation, evil function, equivalent to Pralaya

## Two Terms, One Function

**Saṃhāra** (sam + hṛ = to gather together, to withdraw, to take back) and **Laya** (from lī = to dissolve, to merge) are used interchangeably in most Purāṇic contexts, though Laya emphasizes the quality of merging back into source while Saṃhāra emphasizes the agency of withdrawal. Both refer to the third cosmic function in the Sṛṣṭi-Sthiti-Saṃhāra triad — the reabsorption of the manifest cosmos.

## Saṃhāra as Śiva's Function

In the Trimūrti framework, Saṃhāra is assigned to Śiva. This assignment has generated persistent misreadings in Western contexts where "destroyer" carries negative moral weight. In the Purāṇic cosmological frame:

- Saṃhāra is as necessary as Sṛṣṭi; neither is morally superior
- Without Saṃhāra, there is no Pralaya; without Pralaya, there is no new Sṛṣṭi
- Śiva as Saṃhāra-agent is performing a service to the cosmos, not an act of violence

The Naṭarāja iconography encodes the full cosmological meaning: Śiva's right hand beats the ḍamaru (the drum of creation, Sṛṣṭi), his second right hand shows abhaya mudrā (protection, Sthiti), his upper left hand holds fire (dissolution, Saṃhāra), his lower left hand points to the raised foot (liberation, Anugraha), and his right foot stands on the demon of ignorance (Tirodhāna overcome). All five acts in one image.

## In the Pañcakṛtya Framework

Kashmir Shaivism and Śaiva Siddhānta expand the three Trimūrti functions into five (pañcakṛtya):
1. Sṛṣṭi (emission)
2. Sthiti (maintenance)
3. Saṃhāra (withdrawal)
4. Tirodhāna (veiling, concealment of Śiva's true nature within Māyā)
5. Anugraha (grace, the revelation that removes the veil)

In this framework, Saṃhāra is the third of five acts, all performed by Śiva. The Trimūrti model and the pañcakṛtya model are both valid; they operate at different levels of resolution.

## Saṃhāra and BG 9.7

Bhagavad Gītā 9.7 states: "All beings, O Arjuna, go into My Prakṛti at the end of a Kalpa; I emit them again at the beginning of another Kalpa." This is Saṃhāra-and-Sṛṣṭi in one verse — the beings dissolve back into Brahman's Prakṛti (Avyakta) at Saṃhāra, and are re-emitted in Sṛṣṭi.

## Audience Metaphor

Saṃhāra is like a breath being exhaled. The in-breath (Sṛṣṭi) draws in; the sustained breath (Sthiti) holds; the out-breath (Saṃhāra) releases back to the stillness (Pralaya) before the next in-breath. The breath does not destroy the air; it returns it to the atmosphere. The cosmic cycle is the breathing of Brahman.
