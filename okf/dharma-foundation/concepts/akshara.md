# Akṣara

**Not:** Letter, Alphabet character, Syllable

## What It Actually Means

Literally "imperishable" (*a-kṣara* — that which does not perish) — the eternal sound that reveals the secret of speech. Another word for letter is *Varṇa* (hue or color). The Sanskrit alphabet (*Varṇamālā*) means "garland of colors or hues which an artist uses to paint reality."

The Ṛṣis are said to have *seen*, not just heard, the Vedas — "every letter is heard as a sound with a corresponding visual hue as it manifests." Sanskrit letters are primordial cosmic sounds with intrinsic reality — not arbitrary symbols chosen by convention.

Sri Aurobindo: "Every one of its vowels and consonants has a particular inalienable force which exists by the nature of things and not by development or human choice; these are the fundamental sounds which lie at the base of the Tantric *bīja mantras* and constitute the efficacy of the [Mantra](/concepts/mantra.md) itself."

## Why "Letter" Fails

"Letter" in English is an arbitrary grapheme — 'A' looks like 'A' because of historical accident. *Akṣara* is an imperishable vibration with intrinsic cosmic significance. The English letter 'A' could be replaced by any symbol; the Akṣara 'अ' cannot, because its sound IS its reality.

## Audience Metaphor

An ordinary letter is like a paint-by-number label — the symbol 'A' is just a sticker we all agreed to use, and we could swap it for any other mark and lose nothing. An Akṣara is the opposite: it is like a specific musical note. You cannot relabel middle-C as middle-D and keep the music — the note *is* its frequency. Each Sanskrit phoneme is a note in reality's score, not a sticker on a box.
