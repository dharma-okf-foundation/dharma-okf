# Dharma

**Not:** Religion, Duty, Righteousness, Law, Morality

## What It Actually Means

Dharma operates at multiple simultaneous levels — cosmic, social, and individual — which is precisely why no single English word can capture it.

At the **cosmic level**, Dharma is *ṛta* — the fundamental ordering principle that holds the universe together. It is not a rule imposed from outside; it is the inherent nature of how reality functions.

At the **social level**, Dharma is the ethical and behavioral framework appropriate to one's stage of life (*āśrama*), role (*varṇa*), and circumstances. It is context-dependent, not categorical — what is dharmic in one situation may be adharmic in another.

At the **individual level**, Dharma is *svadharma* — one's own nature, calling, and unique path. The Bhagavad Gītā (3.35): "Better is one's own dharma, though imperfectly performed, than the dharma of another well performed."

Dharma also means the inherent property of a thing — the dharma of fire is to burn, the dharma of water is to flow. In this sense, it precedes ethics entirely and describes the nature of reality itself.

## Why the Substitutes Fail

**"Religion"** implies a belief system with a founder, a book, and a creed. Dharma has no founder, predates any single text, and is discovered (not revealed). You cannot "convert" to Dharma — you can only recognize it.

**"Duty"** strips the cosmic and metaphysical dimensions entirely, reducing Dharma to a social obligation. Duty is imposed; Dharma is inherent.

**"Righteousness"** imports Christian moral absolutes. Dharma is context-sensitive — the Mahābhārata's genius is showing how Dharma shifts depending on circumstance, role, and consequence.

## Audience Metaphor

Dharma is like gravity — it's the fundamental force that keeps everything in its orbit. You don't choose to follow gravity; you can only align with it or fight it. Fighting it doesn't break the law — it breaks you.
