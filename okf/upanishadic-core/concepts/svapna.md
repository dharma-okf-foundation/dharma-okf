# Svapna — स्वप्न

**Not:** illusion, the unconscious, inferior state

## Source

Svapna is analyzed in Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad verse 4. The experiencer in the dream state is Taijasa (तैजस) — "the luminous one" — from Tejas (light, radiance), referring to the Jīva in its inward orientation through the subtle body. Svapna from the root *svap* — to sleep, to dream.

## The Dream State's Structure

In Svapna:
- **Instrument**: Sūkṣma Śarīra (subtle body) — the Prāṇas and the inner instruments (Manas, Buddhi, Ahaṃkāra, Citta)
- **Field**: Sūkṣma objects — dream images, which are modifications of the subtle body itself
- **Experiencer**: Taijasa — the Jīva identified with the subtle body, directed inward (Antaḥprajña)

## The Three-Level Reality in Svapna

Advaita's three-truth framework applies directly here:
- **Prātibhāsika** (apparent reality): dream objects are real while the dream is occurring — the dream fire burns, the dream flight is exhilarating
- **Vyāvahārika** (conventional reality): on waking, dream objects are sublated — "it was only a dream"; waking objects replace them as the operative reality
- **Pāramārthika** (ultimate reality): both dream and waking objects, from the perspective of Brahma-jñāna, are modifications appearing within Brahman

## Why Svapna Is Philosophically Important

The dream state provides the clearest demonstration that the mind can construct an entire coherent world of objects from within itself, without any external input. While dreaming, the dreamer does not know they are dreaming — the experience is as convincing as waking. This has two implications:

1. **For Māyā analysis**: If the mind can project an entire world without external material in dream, the Vedāntic argument for the waking world's appearance-status (Vivartavāda) becomes less counterintuitive. The waking world may similarly be a more stable and persistent projection within Brahman-consciousness.

2. **For the witness inquiry**: In dream, the gross body is inoperative — yet experience continues. This proves that the witness of experience is not the gross body. The investigation uses this as evidence that the Sākṣin is subtler than the gross body.

## Audience Metaphor

A movie director watching their own film gets fully immersed — the fear is real, the joy is real — while the screen and projector remain unchanged behind the experience. When the film ends (waking up), the director remembers: "Those characters were in my film." Neither the immersion nor the remembering erases what actually happened. Svapna is like the immersive experience: fully real in its domain, retrospectively recognized as a production.
