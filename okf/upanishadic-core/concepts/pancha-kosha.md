# Pañcakośa — पञ्चकोश

**Not:** layered anatomy of the soul, Ātman in five modes, hierarchy from lower to higher selves

## Source

Taittirīya Upaniṣad, Brahmānanda Vallī (chapter 2), verses 2.1–5. The text presents five nested Kośas (sheaths), each with its own Prāṇa (sustaining structure) and described as "Brahman" — only to be superseded by a more interior sheath. The teaching is a systematic elimination: each Kośa is presented as if it might be the Ātman, then shown to be insufficient.

## The Five Kośas

| Kośa | Sanskrit | Translation | State correlation | Inquiry result |
|------|----------|-------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Annamaya | अन्नमय | food-made | Jāgrat (gross) | Anātman — dependent on food, born, dies |
| Prāṇamaya | प्राणमय | breath-made | Jāgrat (vital) | Anātman — animating force, not the knower |
| Manomaya | मनोमय | mind-made | Svapna (mind) | Anātman — doubts, desires, changes |
| Vijñānamaya | विज्ञानमय | intellect-made | Jāgrat/Svapna (intellect) | Anātman — discriminates but is still an object |
| Ānandamaya | आनन्दमय | bliss-made | Suṣupti (causal) | Anātman — experienced, witnessed, not the witness |

## The Inquiry Structure

The Taittirīya presents the Kośas as a series of provisional identifications. "The one who knows" is first thought to be the body (Annamaya). But the body cannot be the knower — it is known, it changes, it depends on food. So "the one who knows" must be subtler.

The investigation moves inward through each Kośa. Each time, the proposed Kośa is shown to be an object of the Ātman, not the Ātman itself:
- The Annamaya is perceived; what perceives cannot be only what is perceived
- The Prāṇamaya breathes; what witnesses breathing cannot be the breathing
- The Manomaya doubts and desires; what witnesses doubt cannot be only the doubter
- The Vijñānamaya decides; what witnesses decision cannot be only the decision-maker
- The Ānandamaya is experienced in Suṣupti; what witnesses Suṣupti's bliss cannot be the bliss-sheath

The Ātman is the witness of all five — the one that never becomes an object in the inquiry.

## Relationship to the Three Bodies

The Pañcakośa and the Trayaśarīra (three bodies) models are not competing alternatives — they are complementary analyses:
- **Annamaya Kośa** = Sthūla Śarīra (gross body)
- **Prāṇamaya + Manomaya + Vijñānamaya Kośas** = Sūkṣma Śarīra (subtle body)
- **Ānandamaya Kośa** = Kāraṇa Śarīra (causal body)

The Kośa model is finer-grained within the subtle body domain; the Śarīra model is broader. Both terminate in the Ātman as what is left when all are discarded.

## The Mahāvākya Connection

The Taittirīya's Pañcakośa inquiry ends (2.9) with a declaration: *ānandaṃ brahmeti vyajānāt* — "he recognized Ānanda as Brahman." This is not a conclusion that the Ānandamaya Kośa is Brahman; it is a recognition that the Ānanda that is Brahman's own nature (Svarūpa Lakṣaṇa) was being approximated by each Kośa — and is finally recognized as the unchanging ground beneath them all. The Sat-Cit-Ānanda of Brahman was what was being sought at each layer; the Pañcakośa inquiry removes the search by discarding each imposter.

## Audience Metaphor

A traveler searching for a treasure follows a map that describes the treasure as being inside five concentric boxes. Opening the outermost box (Annamaya), they find a smaller box labeled "this is also not it." Opening the second (Prāṇamaya) — another smaller box, same label. Third (Manomaya), fourth (Vijñānamaya) — same. Opening the fifth (Ānandamaya) — they find not another box but the recognition that the one who was opening all the boxes was never itself a box. The treasure was never in the boxes; it was the one who was searching. The boxes are real — the search was necessary — but the finding is a recognition, not an acquisition.
