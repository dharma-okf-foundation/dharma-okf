# Ānandamaya Kośa — आनन्दमय कोश

**Not:** the Ātman or the true self, the same as Brahman's Ānanda (intrinsic bliss), the causal plane or higher causal body in New Age frameworks

## Etymology

Ānanda (आनन्द) = bliss, joy; maya (मय) = made of, full of, permeated by; Kośa (कोश) = sheath, layer, envelope. The bliss-permeated sheath — the layer of apparent selfhood permeated by bliss, experienced primarily in deep sleep and in states of deep meditative absorption.

## Source and Structure

Taittirīya Upaniṣad 2.5 describes the Ānandamaya Kośa as the fifth and innermost sheath. The text assigns it a structure: Priya (delight) as its head, Moda (joy) as its right wing, Pramoda (great joy) as its left wing, Ānanda (bliss) as its body, and Brahman as its tail (puccha) — the support that the sheath rests on.

The "tail = Brahman" detail is significant: the Taittirīya places Brahman not inside the Ānandamaya Kośa but as its supporting ground — distinct from the sheath, supporting it without being identical with it.

## Why the Ānandamaya Kośa Is Not the Ātman

Vivekacūḍāmaṇi (vv. 218–230) argues this point systematically:

1. **It is a modification (Vikāra)**: The Ānandamaya Kośa is produced by Avidyā — it arises and subsides. Whatever arises and subsides is in the domain of the Vyāvahārika, not the Pāramārthika.

2. **It is experienced as an object**: In Suṣupti, the bliss of the Ānandamaya Kośa is experienced. The Sākṣin (witness) of Suṣupti's bliss is not that bliss — it is what knows the bliss. An experienced object cannot be the subject of experience.

3. **It is associated with Karma**: The Ānandamaya Kośa (= Kāraṇa Śarīra) is the storehouse of Saṃskāras — the causal material of future bodies and experiences. The Ātman has no Karma and accumulates no Saṃskāras.

4. **It is conditioned by states**: The Ānandamaya Kośa is most accessible in Suṣupti and in Samādhi. The Ātman is present in all three states without modification.

## The Suṣupti Connection

The Ānandamaya Kośa is the vehicle of the Prājña experiencer in Suṣupti. When the gross and subtle bodies are at rest in deep sleep, what remains active is the causal body — and the Prājña experiences the undifferentiated bliss of the Ānandamaya Kośa. The retrospective report ("I slept well and was happy") refers to this bliss.

Advaita uses this to make a double point: (1) the Ānandamaya Kośa is real — its bliss is real — but (2) the Sākṣin that witnesses even this bliss is the Ātman, which is not the Ānandamaya Kośa.

## The Practical Edge: Avoiding the Samādhi Error

The Ānandamaya Kośa is the concept that corrects the error of treating Samādhi (deep meditative absorption) as liberation. In high states of Samādhi, the practitioner may experience an overwhelming Ānanda — formless, undifferentiated, beyond all objects. If the Ānandamaya Kośa is mistaken for the Ātman, this state appears to be liberation. But it is still a state — it arises and passes. Liberation (Jīvanmukti) is the recognition of the Ātman that persists through the coming and going of the Samādhi state.

## Audience Metaphor

A jeweler searches for a diamond inside five concentric boxes. The innermost box (the fifth, Ānandamaya) is made of the finest material — nearly transparent, almost glowing. Seeing it, one might think: "Surely the diamond is in THIS box." But opening it, one finds only the finest material of the box — and the realization that the diamond was never in any box. The boxes were always only boxes; the diamond was never contained. The Ānandamaya Kośa's near-transparency to consciousness makes it the most compelling candidate for the Ātman — and therefore the most carefully discarded.
