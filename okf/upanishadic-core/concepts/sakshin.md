# Sākṣin — साक्षिन्

**Not:** the observer, introspection, meditator, individual awareness

## Etymology

Sākṣin (साक्षिन्) = witness, one who sees directly; from sākṣāt (directly, immediately, face to face) + suffix *-in* (one who does). Sākṣāt itself is from sa (with) + akṣa (eye/sense). The witness "sees" in the sense of knowing directly — not through inference, not through the instrument of the mind, but by being the very light in which things appear.

## The Technical Distinction: Sākṣin vs. Pramātā

Advaita distinguishes two aspects of the apparent individual:
- **Pramātā** (the empirical knower): the Jīva functioning as a knowing subject — using the Antaḥkaraṇa (inner instrument), engaging in Vyāvahārika (conventional) knowing of objects
- **Sākṣin** (the witness): the self-luminous awareness in which the Pramātā and its knowing-activity appear as objects

The Pramātā is the "I" of ordinary experience — the one who perceives, thinks, desires, and remembers. The Sākṣin is not this "I." It is that in which this "I" appears. Nididhyāsana gradually erodes the identification with Pramātā and reveals the Sākṣin as the actual referent of "Aham" in the Mahāvākya.

## Self-Luminosity (Svaprakāśatva)

The defining quality of Sākṣin is Svaprakāśatva — self-luminosity. Every object (pot, thought, emotion, state) is known by something other than itself. The pot is known by the eye; the eye is known by the mind; the mind is known by the Buddhi (intellect); the Buddhi is known by the Sākṣin. But the Sākṣin cannot be known by something other than itself without infinite regress. Therefore it must be self-luminous — its knowing-nature does not depend on another knowing.

This self-luminosity is not a property Sākṣin has — it is what Sākṣin IS. And it is precisely what Prajñānam Brahma points to: the luminous knowing-nature is identical with Brahman.

## Sākṣin and the Three States

The Sākṣin is demonstrated through the Avasthātraya inquiry (see avastha-traya.md):
- In Jāgrat: the Sākṣin witnesses the waking Viśva
- In Svapna: the Sākṣin witnesses the dreaming Taijasa
- In Suṣupti: the Sākṣin witnesses the absence of objects — which is how the Suṣupti bliss can be reported retrospectively on waking

The Sākṣin is the functional expression of Turīya in the presence of the three states. When the three states are present, Turīya-as-Sākṣin witnesses them. Turīya itself is the ground; Sākṣin is Turīya as it appears from within the inquiry context.

## Bṛhadāraṇyaka's Antaryāmin Passage

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka's Antaryāmin (3.7.23) provides an early formulation: "He who dwells in consciousness, within consciousness, whom consciousness does not know, whose body consciousness is, who rules consciousness from within — He is your Ātman, the inner ruler, the immortal." This "inner ruler within consciousness but unknown by consciousness" is Sākṣin — it cannot be an object of consciousness because it is the subject that consciousness appears within.

## The Practical Implication for Ātmavicāra

Self-inquiry (Ātmavicāra) uses the Sākṣin as its organizing principle: "Who is the witness of this thought? Who knows this experience?" Each answer ("my mind," "my intellect") can be further questioned ("Who knows the mind?") until no further regression is possible — until the awareness that cannot be objectified is recognized. That irreducible remainder is Sākṣin = Ātman.

## Audience Metaphor

A cinema projector illuminates the screen — the images appear because of the light. The light itself is not in any of the images; you cannot point to it in the film. If all the images are removed, the light remains. If the film shows a lamp, the light of the projector is not in that lamp — yet without the projector's light, the image of the lamp cannot be seen. Sākṣin is the projector-light: the self-luminous source of all experience, itself not appearing as any experience.
