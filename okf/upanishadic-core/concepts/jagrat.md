# Jāgrat — जाग्रत्

**Not:** objective reality, uniquely privileged state for knowledge

## Source

Jāgrat is analyzed in Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad verse 3. The experiencer in the waking state is named Viśva (विश्व) — "the all-pervading one" — referring to the Jīva in its outward orientation. Jāgrat from the root *jāgṛ* — to be awake, to be vigilant.

## The Waking State's Structure

In Jāgrat, experience occurs through:
- **Instrument**: Sthūla Śarīra (gross body) — the physical organism with its fourteen sense-and-action organs
- **Field**: Sthūla objects — gross physical objects, other persons, the external world
- **Experiencer**: Viśva — the Jīva identified with the gross body

The waking state is characterized by Bahiṣprajña — "outward consciousness" — awareness directed toward external objects through the senses.

## Why the Waking State Is Not Privileged

In ordinary experience, waking is treated as "real" and dream as "mere imagination." The Māṇḍūkya's point is subtler: both waking and dream share the same structure — an experiencer (Viśva or Taijasa) experiences a field of objects through an instrument (gross or subtle body). The difference is in the density and type of objects, not in the epistemological status of the experiencing-structure.

The Pāramārthika critique of waking experience is that waking objects are also known only within consciousness — they are not "outside" consciousness any more than dream objects are. The appearance of external objects is the *Vyāvahārika* level of reality — pragmatically real, useful, and conventionally valid, but not the final level from which the Ātman-Brahman inquiry proceeds.

## Jāgrat in the Three-States Inquiry

The inquiry uses Jāgrat as its starting point because it is most familiar. The student is asked: "Who are you in waking?" — the body? (No, the body is seen in waking, therefore known by something other than itself.) The mind? (No, the mind is also known, therefore also an object.) The investigation proceeds until what cannot be objectified is located — the witness. The same analysis is then applied to Svapna and Suṣupti.

## Audience Metaphor

A person wakes up and immediately assumes the waking-world is "the real one." But they have also just returned from a dream in which they were equally certain of their experience. The Māṇḍūkya asks: "What if you apply the same critical scrutiny to the waking world that you now (in waking) apply to the dream?" The uncomfortable answer is that waking objects also appear and disappear, are experienced only within consciousness, and cannot survive the Neti Neti inquiry as the permanent ground of reality.
